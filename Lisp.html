History of Lisp

Lisp is the second eldest programming language in the computer science world, next to Fortran. Lisp is most commonly used for AI and solving complex mathematical problems. The language was created by computer scientist John McCarthy at MIT in 1959 as part of his artificial intelligence research there. After Lisp was created, various individuals and organizations changed Lisp to fit specific computational needs and purposes. These variations of the Lisp language became what we refer to as "dialects." Below are some (but not all) of the dialects that fall into the Lisp category:

  • Common Lisp
  • Arc
  • AutoLisp
  • BBN Lisp
  • Chicken
  • Clojure
  • Emac's Lisp
  • Ikarus
  • InterLisp
  • Racket

Fun fact: Lisp is an acronym for "List Processing."
*No, the name does not refer to the speech impediment*

Where is Lisp Used?

While it is true that Lisp may be rather old, it is not true that the language is useless. Below are some examples of where Lisp is being used currently:

  • Lisp is commonly used in academics as a tool to study AI.
  • Grammarly, a text-editing web service students and professionals use, incorporates Lisp in its code.
  • Boeing commercial aircraft use Lisp-based servers to function.
  • Roomba, an automated vacuum by iRobot, uses a code subset of Common Lisp.
